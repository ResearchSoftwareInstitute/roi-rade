Dell cybersecurity arm slashes IPO price, shares begin trading

SecureWorks Inc., the cybersecurity subsidiary of Dell Inc., has the year’s first tech initial public offering but had to slash its share-price expectations dramatically.

It's a rough start for the Atlanta-based company, which Dell bought in February 2011. SecureWorks raised $120 million after pricing 8 million shares on Thursday at $14, missing its previously indicated $15.50-$17.50 range, CNBC reported, citing an unnamed source. The company was originally aiming to price 9 million shares.

Shares began trading Friday on the Nasdaq under the ticker "SCWX." Reaction was mixed, with the shares moving as high as $14.60 and as low as $13.50 before ending the day flat at $14.

The IPO unlocks value and gives privately held Dell the currency of a publicly traded stock. It is also expected to contribute to Dell's proposed $67 billion merger with EMC Corp. Based in Round Rock, Dell is the largest public company in the Austin area with some 13,000 employees.

The public offering also showcases SecureWorks' increased value under Dell ownership. The IPO reflects a valuation of $1.1 billion, the New York Times reports, more than double the $612 million Dell paid for it.

SecureWorks, led by Michael Cote, monitors corporate networks and helps protect against online attacks from crooks looking to steal financial data, intellectual property and other information.

Dell has since made Atlanta the headquarters of its cybersecurity business and significantly expanded the workforce there. SecureWorks’ Cote is considered a trusted confidant of Dell founder and CEO Michael Dell.

SecureWorks said proceeds from the IPO will be used for working capital and other general corporate purposes. The company reported a net loss of $72.3 million on net revenue of $339.5 million in the fiscal year that ended Jan. 29, 2016.

Publicly traded competitors FireEye and Rapid7 had strong IPOs, but their stocks are now trading at less than the issue prices.

For Dell, the SecureWorks spinoff is a play on white-hot demand for cybersecurity software and services, following a spate of data breaches at Fortune 500 corporations, such as Home Depot Inc., Target, JP Morgan Chase & Co. and Sony Corp.

Cybersecurity remains a strong investment market as a spate of expensive and reputation-busting data breaches at Fortune 500 companies have made securing critical business and customer data from internet hackers the No. 1 priority for CEOs and their boards. The government is also a major driver for the cybersecurity industry. President Barack Obama, in his new budget proposal, is seeking $19 billion for federal cybersecurity initiatives, a 35 percent increase over the previous fiscal year.

This story was updated with the valuation of SecureWorks Inc. in wake of its IPO.
